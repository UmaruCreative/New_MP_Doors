The Craftsman Entry Door is built around one of the most recognizable American residential door profiles — straight-edge panel construction with a glazing bead that frames the glass with the clean horizontal and vertical lines the craftsman style demands. The Materials Behind the Glass

01

CRAFTSMAN DESIGN DETAILS

Straight Edge. Glazing Bead. Correct Proportions.

Craftsman design is defined by its honesty of construction — visible joinery, flat surfaces, clean lines that don't hide the structure of the door. The MP Doors Craftsman delivers that with a straight-edge panel profile and a glazing bead that frames the glass with the same precision the style demands. No decorative oval insert, no arched glass, no ornamental hardware details that conflict with the architectural vocabulary.

02

FINISH OPTIONS

Four Finishes — Wood Tones to Bold Color

Medium Oak — warm mid-tone woodgrain, suits craftsman bungalows with natural wood trim. Dark Walnut — deeper stained woodgrain, more formal, suits homes with darker exterior schemes. White — clean painted finish, suits farmhouse and contemporary craftsman styles. Black — bold painted finish, the highest-contrast curb appeal choice; pairs with any exterior siding color and has become the most popular entry door color in modern renovation. All four are factory applied to composite substrate.

03

GLASS OPTIONS

Four Glass Options — Including Vintage

Low E — standard clear Low-Emissivity glass; maximum light, best thermal performance. Water Wave — rippled texture glass that diffuses light and obscures the interior view while retaining brightness. Frosted — fully opaque finish, maximum privacy, full light transmission. Vintage — period-appropriate glass texture that suits the craftsman era; light diffusion with a visual character that reads as intentional on historical styles. Also available with a 3 Lite SDL configuration for Low E, Frosted, and Water Wave — three-panel simulated divided lite grid for a more traditional craftsman appearance. 04

COMPOSITE CONSTRUCTION

Craftsman Proportions. Composite Durability.

Traditional craftsman entry doors came in Douglas fir or oak — species that hold stain well but require refinishing every few years and are vulnerable to moisture swelling in the humid climates where craftsman bungalows were most commonly built (the Pacific Northwest, the Southeast, the upper Midwest). The MP Doors Craftsman keeps the profile and the four-finish palette of those historical doors on composite construction that won't swell, rot, or ask for refinishing. Frequently Asked Questions

Answers verified before publishing — [VERIFY] items noted throughout

What home styles suit the Craftsman Entry Door? +
The Craftsman Entry Door is built for craftsman bungalows, American foursquares, farmhouse-style homes, and contemporary craftsman construction. The straight-edge panel profile and glazing bead are the defining visual elements of craftsman door design — they fit homes where the architecture emphasizes structure, horizontal lines, and natural materials. The door is also appropriate for transitional homes that mix craftsman detail with modern simplicity. It is not the right choice for traditional colonial, Victorian, or formal classical homes where a paneled or oval-insert entry door is expected.
What is the 3 Lite SDL variant — and when should I choose it? +
The 3 Lite SDL (Simulated Divided Lite) version of the Craftsman door adds a three-panel grid overlay to the glass — it creates the appearance of three separate glass panels divided by structural bars, which is a traditional craftsman door detail. Choose the 3 Lite SDL when your home has sidelites or windows with a divided-lite grid and you want the front door to match that pattern. It is available with Low E, Frosted, and Water Wave glass. [VERIFY: confirm 3 Lite SDL is available for all three listed glass options and confirm from Home Depot listing before publishing.]
Is the Black finish available — and does it hold up in sun exposure? +
Yes — Black is one of four available finishes and is factory applied to composite substrate. The composite doesn't expand or contract with temperature changes the way steel does, so the black finish doesn't chip or peel from the metal expansion-contraction cycle that causes maintenance issues on black-painted steel doors in hot climates. [VERIFY: confirm UV and heat performance data for the Black finish on this composite model — black surfaces absorb more solar heat than lighter colors; confirm whether any specific performance testing or warranty language addresses this.]
What is Vintage glass — and why does it suit craftsman homes? +
Vintage glass has a texture and visual character that references early 20th century architectural glass — the type used in craftsman bungalows and foursquares built between roughly 1905 and 1930. It diffuses light and creates a period-appropriate appearance that works with the horizontal line emphasis of craftsman architecture. It's the glass choice that looks intentional on a historical craftsman home rather than modern clear glass or contemporary frosted options. [VERIFY: confirm exact Vintage glass description and privacy level from Home Depot listing or manufacturer spec before publishing.]
What is the difference between Water Wave and Frosted glass? +
Both Water Wave and Frosted obscure the interior view. Water Wave uses a rippled or wavy texture — it diffuses light and blocks the clear view while maintaining some visual interest in the glass surface itself. Frosted uses a uniform matte-white surface — fully opaque, no visual texture beyond the color, maximum privacy. Frosted suits entries that face the street directly or where privacy is the primary concern. Water Wave suits entries where some visual texture in the glass is preferred.
