What is the difference between the Shaker Gliding and the Shaker Hinged 2/3 Lite? +
Both are Shaker Series doors with the same 2/3 lite glass format — glass across the upper two-thirds, solid composite lower third. The operating difference is how the door opens. The Shaker Gliding slides on a track; the Shaker Hinged 2/3 Lite swings on hinges (with both panels operable). The Gliding requires no swing clearance at the interior or exterior — appropriate when floor space adjacent to the opening is limited. The Hinged version gives a wider clear opening (no fixed panel blocking the frame) and no track to maintain, but requires swing clearance. The Gliding also offers a screen and three handle finishes as options; the Hinged offers a pet door but does not list a screen.

Why stainless steel rollers instead of standard steel? +
Standard steel track hardware corrodes over time — particularly in humid climates, coastal areas with salt air, or any climate with significant temperature cycling. A corroded roller doesn't glide cleanly; eventually the door becomes hard to operate, and replacing track hardware after installation is a labor-intensive fix. Stainless steel is corrosion-resistant under the same conditions. The rollers are also adjustable — if the door shifts out of level over time (common in homes that settle), you can realign the door in the track without replacing hardware.
How do I choose between the three handle finishes? +
Match to your existing interior hardware — specifically the hardware on adjacent interior doors, kitchen cabinets, or bathroom fixtures in the same visual zone as the patio door. White hardware coordinates with white painted trim and a clean contemporary look. Satin nickel is the most broadly neutral — it reads as a warm silver that works with stainless appliances, nickel cabinet hardware, and transitional finishes. Brown works with oil-rubbed bronze and dark hardware profiles. Choose the finish at purchase — it ships as part of the door unit and is not a field-installed accessory that can be swapped later without affecting the handle mechanism.

What's the auxiliary flush bolt — and do I need it? +
The keyed handle lock secures the sliding panel against the fixed frame at the latch point. The auxiliary flush bolt is a secondary lock — typically a top-mounted bolt that engages into the header track — providing a second independent point of security on the same panel. For a back-facing patio door, two locking points mean a forced entry attempt requires defeating both simultaneously. The auxiliary flush bolt is already included in the door; there's no separate purchase decision to make, but it's worth knowing it's there and using it.
